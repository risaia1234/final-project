Genetic Basis of Chromosomal Disorders

By Shaakir Farah

Chromosomal Disorders are conditions caused by defects in ones chromosomes. My topic explores what types of chromosomal disorders there are and how they form at a cellular level. For that, we need some background knowledge.

Lets start at the beginning. Gametes are haploid cells that are used for sexual reproduction. One gamete from the male and one from the female to create a diploid. For males, its called sperm and for females, its called an egg. The process in which Gametes are made is called Meiosis. Most organisms have 2 sets of chromosomes in there somatic cells but gametes have one set.


Mitosis is the process in which cells split and result in somatic cells. Those cells are diploids. Meiosis is the process of splitting diploids into haploids. Instead of the cell having two sets of chromosomes like in mitosis, they have one set of chromosomes that are used in sexual reproduction.

Meiosis comes in two parts. Meiosis 1 and Meiosis 2.

  1. Meiosis 1 is where a diploid germ cell splits into two diploid daughter cells.
    • Interphase: Chromosomes need to duplicate creating sister chromatids.
    • Prophase I: Homologous chromosomes pair together forming tetrads. This formation of tetrads is called Synapsis. Then, a process called crossing over occurs. Segments of DNA are exchanged between homologous chromosomes creating more genetic variation.
    • Metaphase I: Microtubules move the tetrads to the metaphase plate (in the middle of the two centrioles).
    • Anaphase I: Tthe tetrads split apart but keep the sister chromatids together.
    • Telophase I: The cell splits into 2 diploid daughter cells.
  2. Meiosis 2 is where the daughter cells from Meiosis 1 get there sister chromatids separated as well forming four haploid cells.
    • Metaphase II: The sister chromatids meet at the metaphase plate.
    • Anaphase II: The sister chromatids split up and move towards the poles of the cell.
    • Telophase II: The cell divides and as a result, 4 haploid cells form.

Chromosomal Disorders stem from many different anomalys but translocation, nondisjunction, and inversion are the most common.

Nondisjunction is when the chromosomes don’t properly separate. It can happen in Meiosis 1 or Meiosis 2.

Translocation is when a segment of a chromosome breaks off and reattaches itself either on another chromosome or on the chromosome it broke off of. There are 2 types of translocation. Reciprocal translocation and non-reciprocal translocation.


Inversion is when a part of a chromosome detaches, flips around 180° and reattaches. If a chromosome includes the detachment of the centromere, then it’s called pericentric and if it doesn’t include it, then it’s paracentric.
